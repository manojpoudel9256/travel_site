Our Story

Born in the Shadow of Sagarmatha

Sagarmatha — "the head of the sky" — is what we call Everest in Nepal. Our founders grew up beneath it, herding yaks on the trails you'll trek. This company is our way of sharing home.

EVEREST Expeditions began in 2004 with two brothers from Khumjung village, a borrowed office desk in Thamel, and a single trekking group of four. Pemba Sherpa had already carried loads through the Khumbu Icefall for foreign expeditions; he believed Nepali guides shouldn't just carry the ropes — they should lead the way.

Two decades later, our guides — every one of them from the Solukhumbu region — have led more than 300 expeditions and treks, from family walks to Namche Bazaar to the summit of Sagarmatha itself. We remain proudly Nepali-owned and Kathmandu-based.

When the 2015 earthquake struck, our teams spent the season rebuilding trails and teahouses instead of guiding. That choice cost us a year of business and earned us something better: the trust of every village along the route.

Milestones

Twenty Years of Trail Dust

2004

Founded in Thamel, Kathmandu by Pemba and Lakpa Dorje Sherpa of Khumjung village. First season: 11 trekkers.

2008

Our first full Everest expedition puts four clients and five Sherpas on the summit via the South Col.

2015

After the Gorkha earthquake we suspend commercial trips for the season, rebuilding trails, bridges, and teahouses across Solukhumbu.

2019

Mingma Yangzi Sherpa becomes our first woman lead guide, heading the season's biggest Base Camp departure.

2024

We celebrate our 300th expedition — and our guides' 100th combined summit of Sagarmatha.

What we stand on

Values at Altitude

Safety Before Summits

No summit is worth a life. Our guides hold the authority — and the obligation — to turn any climb around, no questions asked.

Sherpa-Led, Always

Local leadership isn't a marketing line — it's our founding principle. Every EVEREST trip is led, planned, and profited by people of the Khumbu.

Leave Only Footprints

We pack out everything we carry in, follow porter-welfare guidelines for loads and equipment, and fund clean-up treks every season.
